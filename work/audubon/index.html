Case study

Audubon — Migratory Bird Initiative validation.

A two-phase validation engagement against the National Audubon Society's hemispheric bird-migration science platform. The decision was not "should we build this" — donor agreements had foreclosed that. The decision was how: which audiences to build for, which features to prioritize, and what narrative formula would translate science into action.

Industry

Conservation / non-profit

Engagement

Migratory Bird Initiative (MBI)

Duration

Jan – Apr 2020 (2-phase)

Scope

$69K third-party expenses

The challenge.

Audubon had committed — through donor agreements with the Doolin Foundation ($5M) and Joe Ellis ($1M) — to build a hemispheric science platform aggregating spatial data on every North American migratory bird species. The MBI Science team had spent eighteen months building the data foundation. The work had to land with seven distinct audiences (scientists, educators, conservation activists, birders, bird-curious civilians, families, environmentalists) without sacrificing the underlying data integrity — and inside a non-profit governance structure spanning national headquarters, state offices, chapters, and a politically-polarized membership.

The solution — two phases, one framework.

Pair-rank diagnostic

90-statement assumption pair-rank run through both Audubon and Prota teams. The diff surfaced a sharp policy/threats vs audience-segmentation/bird-love divergence in week one — before any test went live.

Qualitative interviews

Academic scientists (Peter Marra), state-office leaders (Sarah Rose, ED Audubon California), chapter presidents, bird-curious civilians, and Cornell Lab of Ornithology leadership.

Data & tech audit

The platform pipeline was well-organized but monolithic — no central database, manual ETL, no statistical modeling, no automated audit / lineage.

Results.

Audubon now ships an interactive bird migration explorer at explorer.audubon.org/home — the live v1 platform that the validation work shaped.

Annual Cycle Maps failed with non-scientists

The most-prototyped MBI feature — Annual Cycle Maps — tested poorly with non-scientist audiences. Sarah Rose called them "very sciencey, with no call to action." Connections Explorer (where birds in my place go) tested better because it's locally personalizable.

Scientist data contribution depends on credit, not licensing fees

The data-sharing economy of migration science is reputational. Audubon's pair-rank had ranked the underlying claim mid-pack — exposing an internal blind spot Prota's qualitative work surfaced clearly.

Political polarization was the biggest engagement risk

Many birders sit on the U.S. political right; framing matters. The qualitative work surfaced this as the durable risk, not data quality or platform completeness.

Methodology lessons.

The Audubon engagement is the canonical reference for two patterns in our methodology library:

  • Pair-rank divergence as kickoff diagnostic — running the same statement set through both client and Prota teams and diffing the priorities surfaces theory-of-value misalignment in week one.
  • Donor-locked / politically-committed roadmaps reframe the engagement value proposition — the work shifts from kill / commit to reshape / de-risk. Future engagements with foundations and innovation arms should price and frame this distinction explicitly.
